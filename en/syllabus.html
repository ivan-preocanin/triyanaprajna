Class topics and reading materials

    At first glance, the list of literature for each semester may seem overwhelming. However, the reading materials are generally not more than one or two chapters from the assigned books per class. The purpose of studies, as has already been mentioned, is not to create an intellectual overload, but to develop understanding and the practical application of knowledge. Before each semester, students will receive a detailed syllabus with reading materials for every class.

Class topics:

  1.     Introduction into genuine spirituality: Sabbāsava sutta

    • How to discover one’s own path through the thicket of spiritual materialism. Ahiṃsā or nonviolence as the foundational principle of Dharma.

  2.     The Four Noble Truths I: Dhammacakkappavattana sutta

    • The truth about our common experience, and the truth about the causes of our troubles and worries.

  3.     The Four Noble Truths II

    • The truth about the medicine without expiration date, and the truth about its application and dosage.

  4.     The four marks of existence I

    • The three types of struggles and problems, plus the impermanence of all things that change, although we wish them not to change.

  5.     The four marks of existence II: Anattalakkhaṇa sutta

    • Not only that things change, but we hold onto something which is not there: nonself and nirvana.

  6.     Śamatha-Vipaśyanā I: Ānāpānasati sutta

    • Calming down and recollecting; the nine stages of the process; obstacles on the path and the results of mindfulness.

  7.     Śamatha-Vipaśyanā II

    • Growing awareness, opening, and letting go.

  8.     The Four Foundations of Mindfulness I: Satipatṭhāna sutta

    • How to recollect through embodying and feeling.

  9.     The Four Foundations of Mindfulness II

    • How to recollect within mind and into what happens in mind.

  10.     The four upside-down views and the eight worldly concerns

    • The discipline of giving up attachment, aversion, and ignorance as the foundation of practice.

  11.     The Four Immeasurables I: Mettā sutta

    • Developing friendliness and caring; opening to a vulnerable space.

  12.     The Four Immeasurables II

    • How to find joy even when wretched; how to find balance in the midst of irritations and worries.

Class topics:

  1.     Introduction to Abhidharma: map of the path through relative existence.

  2.     Five collections of experience (skandha): map No. 1.

  3.     Twelve fields of perception (āyatana),), eighteen units of experience (dhatu): maps No. 2 and No. 3.

  4.     The first level of meditation on emptiness: analytical meditation on the eighteen dhatus. Initial scouting of the terrain.

  5.     Introduction into the elements of the entire experience (dharma) and the functioning of mind as the central mirror: a precise map of the inner landscape.

  6.     Research and analysis of the seventy-five elements of experience: where is the road, and where are the valleys and heights.

  7.     Wholesome and unwholesome elements as disturbing and peaceful instants: treacherous places with a high possibility of accidents and resting places along the road.

  8.     Karma: actions and the result of actions; dynamics of causes, conditions, and consequences. Six sorts of reality. An erial view of the terrain.

  9.     Interdependent origination (paṭicca-samuppāda): the wheel of samsara bumping down the road.

  10.     The five collections of experience from a nonconceptual viewpoint I: Glimpses of abhidharma and the open door to emptiness. How to travel with courage, giving up map or direction.

  11.     The five collections of experience from a nonconceptual viewpoint II: Path without goal, to travel is the goal.

  12.     Taking refuge in Buddha, Dharma and Sangha. Help on the road!

Literature:

Framework of topics:

  • Heart of Dharma: Suññataloka sutta, Prajñāparāmitā hṛdaja sūtra

    • Dharma liberated from any concept of Dharma.

  • Introduction to Mahāyāna I

    • The path of the bodhisattva, compassion and wisdom; bodhicitta in its relative aspect; the practice of sending and taking.

  • Introduction to Mahāyāna II: Prajñāparāmitā in 8000 lines

    • Bodhicitta in its ultimate aspect. Anutpattika dharma kṣānti: taking joy in nonarising of dharmas.

  • Vimalakīrti nirdeśasūtra I

    • Arhats and Bodhisattvas perplexed: Is the householder at home?

  • Vimalakīrti nirdeśasūtra II

    • The house goddess teachings: every presumption of reality is devoid of reality.

  • Entering the way of the bodhisattva; Bodhicaryāvatāra.

      First five pāramitās, transcendental virtues:

    1. Transcendental generosity: how to give it all while giving nothing to no one.
    2. Transcendental discipline: living as a decent human while being void of being.
    3. Transcendental patience: nothing rises, nothing ceases, no one abides.
    4. Transcendental enthusiasm: effortless liberation of non-being.
    5. Transcendental meditation: sitting practice with no ground to sit on.

  • Bodhisattva in action: Examples of mind training. Composting lojong instructions into one’s own experience.

  • Gaṇḍavyūha sūtra: travails of the bodhisattva Suddhana through the Realm of Reality. Limitless aspirations of a bodhisattva for limitless sentient beings.

Framework of topics:

  • Mūlamadhyamakakārikā I

    • The basic understanding of the Middle Way between the extremes of assertions and conclusions. Deconstructing intellect through sharpening the intellect.

  • Mūlamadhyamakakārikā II

    • The inseparability of samsara and nirvana. Accepting the relativity of viewpoints on the path to dismissing them all.

  • What we dream with open eyes: progressive stages on the meditation on emptiness.

    • What we confabulate and what really is; relative and ultimate truth.

  • The four schools of relative and ultimate truth:

    • Sautrāntika: What is just a story, and what can perform some work.

    • Cittamātra: Mind over matter.

    • Svatantrika: Expressing the inexpressible with a little help of autonomous friends.

    • Prasaṅgika: Refusal to express the inexpressible. Exposing concepts as an expression of an attachment to concepts.

  • Thirty-seven practices of a Bodhisattva.

    • A short overview of the path of a bodhisattva; the union of compassion and wisdom.

  • The ninth chapter of Shatideva’s “The way of the bodhisattva” (Bodhicaryāvatāra).

    • A commentary by Mipham Rinpoche. The Four Foundations of Mindfulness from the Madhyamaka standpoint.

Third year: The Nature of Enlightenment

Semester V: Buddha nature

Foundational literature:

Literature:

    Khenchen Thrangu Rinpoche, The Uttara Tantra: A Treatise on Buddha Nature, Sri Satguru Publications, 1994.

    Thich Nhat Hanh, Understanding Our Mind: 50 Verses on Buddhist Psychology, Parallax Press, 2006.

Framework of topics:

  • The Third Turning of the Wheel of Dharma and Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra.

    • Guiding (neyartha) and definitive (nītārhta) meaning.

  • Yogacāra tradition and the meaning of “practice in action.”

    • Three natures of perception or what creates the movie called “My Life.” Āśrayaparāvṛtti: the complete transformation of the basis of consciousness.

  • How to discern eight types of consciousness from awareness.

    • Ālāyavijnjāna or root consciousness versus ālāya, unlimited awareness.

  • Uttaratantra śāstra and Buddha nature.

    • Finally we can exhale. Knowingly (vidya) or not knowingly (avidyā) we are enlightened without beginning or end.

  • The seven indestructible points of Buddha nature as the foundation of unwavering confidence.

  • The nine similes about the indestructible element: a diamond in the mud along the road.

  • Tāranātha, The Essence of Shentong.

    • Cula suññata Sutta and Śūñatā nāma mahāsūtra. The Shentong view of the emptiness of other. The tradition of the Great Madhyamaka. The emptiness of other is the fullness of Buddha mind.

Framework of topics:

  • Embarking a supersonic vehicle. The open heart of bodhicitta as the wellspring and aspiration of the journey.

  • Tantra as a practical approach to Buddha nature. A method of coming in touch, experiencing, and identifying with enlightened being.

  • Creation and completion: two levels of tantric practice. Śamatha and vipaśyanā revisited.

  • Creation of a Pure Land as a result of enlightened action. Long and short Sukhāvatīvyūha Sūtra. Buddha Amitābha at work.

  • Five Budha families and the transformation of the five skandhas.

  • A few stories about our teachers and their journeys: Tilo, Naro, Marpa, Mila, and the boyz from the hood. Critical intelligence rather than blind faith as the basis of devotion and relationship with the teacher.

  • Enlightened expressions of the female masters of Dharma: from Therīgāthā to Yeshe Tsogyal and Machig Labdrön.

  • Songs of complete realization: Theragāthā, Saraha, Tilopa, Milarepa, The Rain of Wisdom.

  • Mahāmudrā i Mahā Ati from a pure theoretical standpoint, which is practically impossible.
